The Monty Hall problem is a probability puzzle based on a game show scenario. Here's why switching doors increases your chances of winning:

1. **Initial Choice**: When you first choose one door out of three, there's a 1/3 chance that the car (the prize) is behind your chosen door and a combined 2/3 chance that it's behind one of the other two doors.

2. **Host Opens a Door**: After you make your initial choice, the host, who knows what's behind each door, opens another door from the remaining unchosen ones that doesn't have the car. This action provides additional information.

3. **Switching vs. Sticking**: If you decide to stick with your original choice, your odds remain at 1/3 (since nothing has changed about which door initially had the prize). However, if you switch to one of the other two doors that were not opened by the host, your chances improve dramatically.

4. **Probability Shift**: The key insight is that when the host opens a door without the car, they effectively consolidate all the 2/3 probability (from choosing incorrectly initially) into the remaining unopened door(s). Thus, switching gives you a 2/3 chance of winning the car, compared to your original 1/3.

In essence, by switching doors after one is opened, you're leveraging the higher initial probability that was not in your favor. This counterintuitive result highlights how additional information (the host's action) can significantly alter probabilities, making switching the optimal strategy.
